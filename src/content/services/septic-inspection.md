A routine septic inspection is a general checkup on your system performed outside the formal Title 5 process. It's something you might schedule if you've just bought a home that recently passed a Title 5 inspection but you want a second opinion, if you notice signs of a problem like slow drains or soft wet spots in the yard, or simply as part of regular home maintenance. This type of inspection is less formal than a Title 5 and gives you a current picture of how your system is holding up.

During a routine inspection, a technician will typically look at the accessible tank components — checking baffles, inlet and outlet conditions, sludge and scum levels if the tank is pumped, and the condition of the distribution box if accessible. They'll walk the leach field area and look for signs of saturation, surfacing effluent, or unusual vegetation patterns that can indicate a failing field. They'll note anything that looks concerning and tell you whether you should be watching something closely or scheduling repairs.

A routine inspection doesn't produce a formal Title 5 report and doesn't satisfy the state's inspection requirement for property transfers. But it can give you peace of mind, catch small problems before they become expensive ones, and help you plan for future maintenance. It's especially useful if you're five or more years out from your last formal inspection and want to know how things are looking before committing to a sale or a home addition.

Costs are generally lower than a formal Title 5 inspection — typically $150–$400 depending on what's included and how much of the system is accessible. If a pump-out is also performed during the visit, that's priced separately. Not every septic contractor offers standalone inspections; some only do them in conjunction with pumping or as part of a formal Title 5 process. It's worth asking when you call what's covered and what the deliverable looks like — a written summary is more useful than a verbal report if you need to share the findings with a real estate agent or contractor later.
