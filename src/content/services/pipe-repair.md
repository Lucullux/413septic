The pipes that connect your home's plumbing to the septic tank, and the pipes between the tank and the leach field, are the circulatory system of your septic setup. When those pipes fail — through cracking, root intrusion, joint separation, or collapse — sewage can't flow properly, and you'll typically see the symptoms in your home first: slow drains, gurgling, or sewage backing up into basement fixtures.

Older homes often have cast iron or clay tile pipes that have been in the ground for 50 years or more. These are prone to cracking, tree root intrusion, and joint failure. Roots are attracted to the warmth and moisture of septic pipes and can infiltrate even hairline cracks, eventually blocking flow entirely. Newer systems use schedule 40 PVC pipe, which is far more durable, but even PVC can be damaged by frost, vehicle traffic over the pipe run, or improper installation.

Diagnosing a pipe problem typically starts with a camera inspection — a flexible cable with a camera head is fed through the pipe to identify the location and nature of the blockage or damage. Once the problem is located, repair options depend on the severity. A localized crack or root intrusion might be cleared with jetting and a spot repair. A section of collapsed or severely deteriorated pipe requires excavation and replacement of that section. In some cases, the entire inlet or outlet pipe needs to be replaced.

Repair costs range widely — $300–$2,000 depending on the length of pipe involved, depth of burial, soil conditions, and whether the problem is a simple clearing or a full pipe replacement. Camera inspection may be billed separately or included in the diagnostic visit. If you're in an older home in Franklin County with original cast iron or clay drainage pipes, it's worth having those pipes inspected proactively as part of any major maintenance visit — catching a deteriorating pipe before it fully fails is almost always cheaper than responding to an emergency backup.
