An effluent filter is a device installed in the outlet baffle of your septic tank that filters out small solid particles before the liquid effluent flows out to your leach field. Not all systems have one — they're more common in newer systems or systems that have been recently upgraded — but if your system has one, keeping it clean is one of the simplest and most cost-effective things you can do to protect your leach field from premature failure.

Over time, the filter accumulates solids and can become partially or fully clogged. When the filter is clogged, effluent can't drain from the tank at the normal rate. This causes the liquid level in the tank to back up, which in turn causes slow drains throughout the house and can eventually lead to sewage backing up into the lowest drain in your home. A clogged effluent filter can mimic the symptoms of a failing leach field, so it's worth checking the filter before assuming the worst about your system.

Cleaning the filter is a straightforward process. The filter cartridge is pulled from its housing in the outlet baffle, rinsed with a hose back into the tank (so the solids go back into the tank rather than onto the ground), and reinstalled. This is most efficiently done at the same time as a pump-out, since the tank is already open and the solids being rinsed off the filter will be removed along with the rest of the tank contents. Some homeowners are comfortable doing this themselves; others prefer to have their pumper handle it.

The cost is minimal — typically $50–$200 if done as a standalone service, and often just an add-on charge during a regular pump-out. If your filter hasn't been cleaned in several years, or if you're not sure your system has one, ask your pumper to check the outlet baffle area the next time they're on site. The few dollars it costs to clean a filter is nothing compared to the cost of a leach field repair caused by solids passing through a clogged or absent filter.
